Health Debate Bounces From Death Panels to Death Card

By Jonathan Weisman

Jonathan Weisman reports on the White House.

First came the death panels. Now comes the death card.

After declaring at least partial victory over the charge that an end-of-life counseling provision in health care legislation would create “death panels,” the White House has a new worry. Emails flashing around the nation charge that an obscure section of the House health care bill on the standardization of electronic administrative transactions will somehow lead to, well, death.

Part of the section pertains to the information that private insurers would have to put on those cards they issue to consumers that now contain little more than a group identification and an account number. If insurance cards also had information on what the holder’s plan does and does not cover, the provision’s authors figure doctors and nurses would spend less time on the phone with the insurer asking that question.

That’s not how others see it. “If my card reads that I have a certain condition and the machine has no ‘code’ for that condition, the machine will refer me a ‘consultant’. This person will be compassionate and caring but can not make the machine accept my card so she tells me all about planned deaths, assisted suicides etc. and that they certainly will not let me suffer,” a new email reads.

This is a bit of a head scratcher for a dull section designed to simplify transactions on claims, enrollment and prior authorizations between insurers and health care providers. But perhaps the authors of the bill could have used simpler language in their simplification dictates.

The section in question states for one of its standardization goals, “describe all data elements (such as reason and remark codes) in unambiguous terms, not permit optional fields, require that data elements be either required or conditioned upon set values in other fields, and prohibit additional conditions. … Harmonize all common data elements across administrative and clinical transaction standards.”

Clear enough for ya?
