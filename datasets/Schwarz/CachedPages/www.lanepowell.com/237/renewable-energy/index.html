Renewable Energy

With rising prices of crude oil, continued demand for new energy sources and increasing concern about greenhouse-gas emissions associated with traditional energy production, our nation’s methods of energy generation, transmission and distribution are under unprecedented scrutiny.

New technologies, funding sources and industry activism all are increasing. Federal and state governments are providing renewable energy incentives and, at the same time, many are requiring utilities to include mandated levels of renewable energy in meeting customer requirements. For energy developers, buyers and sellers, these new opportunities and requirements are creating more contract and financing complexity, regulatory oversight and contract litigation, all requiring knowledgeable and experienced legal assistance.

A Range of Pertinent Legal Disciplines

Lane Powell’s interdisciplinary practice assists independent power producers and wholesalers, project developers, investors, lenders and utilities in a variety of ways related to energy financing, development and dispute resolution. Examples include:

  • Project Finance: Lane Powell’s Renewable Energy lawyers represent clients in energy project financing for development, operation, acquisition and disposition of power generation and other industrial and infrastructure matters involving complex financing structures. Lane Powell is one of only a handful of firms in the United States skilled in combining various tax-credit financing programs, such as renewable energy tax credits, new markets tax credits and historic preservation tax credits.
  • Power Purchase Agreements: Lane Powell’s attorneys represent sellers and purchasers of renewable energy in negotiating and drafting power purchase agreements and related credit support documentation, such as guarantees, letters of credit and cash collateral agreements.
  • Project Development. Lane Powell’s Renewable Energy Law attorneys are knowledgeable in energy project development, capable of expertly guiding clients through the real estate, environmental and land-use entitlement aspects of acquiring, siting and permitting an energy facility. The Firm also provides due diligence analysis on acquiring and developing electric-generation and transmission assets for companies throughout the United States.
  • Regulatory: In addition to energy project development and finance, Lane Powell attorneys work with clients on regulatory and transactional matters, as well as energy-related litigation. The Firm’s Renewable Energy Law attorneys have proven experience representing independent power producers and industries in Oregon, Washington and California before state and federal courts and administrative agencies, including the Oregon Public Utility Commission, Washington Utilities and Transportation Commission and Bonneville Power Administration.
  • Litigation and Administrative Proceedings: Lane Powell has expertise in negotiating and litigating on behalf of energy and utility clients, which typically involves both regulatory and environmental challenges. The Firm’s attorneys have worked on cases arising from the California energy crisis litigation and have obtained positive results for clients.

Lane Powell believes that development of technologically advanced, dependable and sustainable sources of energy is critical to our nation’s economic success and security and to preservation of the global environment. Lane Powell strives to stay at the forefront of this developing area and to assist clients in capitalizing on ever-expanding opportunities.

11.09.2009

Oregon Department of Energy Issues Temporary Rules Modifying the Business Energy Tax Credit Program

Oregon’s Department of Energy (“Department”) has issued temporary rules to modify the Business Energy Tax Credit (“BETC”) program for renewable energy projects and to address criticisms about the breadth of the credits. The rules are effective from November 3, 2009, until May 1, 2010.

Gov. Kulongoski requested a review of the BETC rules in early August 2009 when he vetoed a house bill that would have significantly reduced the tax credit for larger scale energy generation projects. The Governor also signed into law two related measures – one directing the Department to conduct an economic analysis on renewable energy projects that qualify for the BETC to better understand the state’s return on the investment in the BETC program, and the other scheduling the BETC program for sunset in 2012, if the legislature does not extend the program by the end of the 2011 session. The Department plans to issue permanent BETC rules after the 2010 special session is completed.

08.14.2009

IRS Announces Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Tax Credit Program: Preliminary Applications Due September 16, 2009

The Internal Revenue Service yesterday issued Notice 2009-72 (the “Program Guidance”) establishing a program implementing the Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Credit (“QAEP Credit”) under Section 48C of the Internal Revenue Code (the “Code”). The Program Guidance may be found on the IRS website: http://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-09-72.pdf.

The QAEP Credit was enacted as part of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 (“ARRA”). It is intended to encourage the development of a domestic manufacturing base to support the U.S. renewable energy industry by providing a tax credit equal to 30 percent of the “qualified investment” in a “qualifying advanced energy project.” The QAEP Credit is subject to a nationwide limitation of $2.3 billion, which must be allocated among eligible projects. ARRA required the Treasury Department to establish by August 17, 2009, a program to consider and award certifications for the QAEP Credit. The program announced yesterday fulfills that mandate and sets a short timeline for credit allocation applications.

07.14.2009

U.S. Treasury Department Issues Guidance for Cash Grants in Lieu of Energy Tax Credits

On July 9, 2009, the U.S. Treasury Department issued guidance for the award of cash grants by the federal government (“Grants”) in lieu of energy tax credits under Section 1603 of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 (“ARRA”). When ARRA was enacted in February 2009, Congress recognized that current economic conditions have reduced investors’ taxable income, and the attractiveness of the production tax credit (“PTC”) and the investment tax credit (“ITC”) as incentives for development of renewable energy projects has diminished. The goal in providing cash grants for a limited period is to spur renewable energy development, even in the absence of taxable income, and to further the goals of job creation and expansion of renewable energy sources.

The Oregon Business Energy Tax Credit

Kimmelfield, Neil

The Oregon Business Energy Tax Credit (“BETC”) is a nonrefundable credit against Oregon personal and corporate income taxes based on the “certified cost” of certain investments in energy conservation, recycling, renewable energy resources, or reduced use of polluting transportation fuels. Originally enacted in 1979, the BETC was significantly expanded in 2007, and expanded again in 2008, as part of an effort to encourage alternative energy development. The BETC is available only for investments in facilities that are completed by the end of 2015. View full article (PDF).

07.14.2006

Portland Becomes First City to Adopt Renewable Fuel Standard

On July 5, 2006, the City of Portland, Oregon, took an aggressive step towards energy independence by passing a ordinance requiring all diesel sold after June 30, 2007, within the City of Portland to contain a minimum blend of 5 percent biodiesel, commonly referred to as “B5,” and all gasoline sold after June 30, 2007, within the City of Portland to contain a minimum blend of 10 percent ethanol, commonly referred to as “E10.” While Minnesota and Washington both have state laws requiring fuels sold in those states to contain a minimum percentage of renewable fuels, Portland is the first city to have a renewable fuel standard. It is expected that the Oregon legislature will pass renewable fuel standards state-wide in 2007. View full article (PDF).
