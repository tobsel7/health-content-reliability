Now Is a Good Time to Invest

Thursday January 22, 2009

Investing money is the way that you really begin to build wealth. When you save money, you begin to set money aside for a rainy day. It can add to your net worth, but you are not making your money work for you. Instead you are simply working for your money. Investing is how you get your money to begin working for you.

Investing can seem intimidating if you have never done it before. One of the easiest ways to invest is by choosing mutual funds. These investments spread the risk over several individual stocks. It is also important to leave the money you have invested alone during down times in the market. In fact, investing more during down times is a good idea. You can purchase more shares for your money. The market is going to recover before you retire. Investing now is like getting everything half off at the store.

It is important for you to be ready to invest your money. Outside of your 401(k) and IRA investments you should not being investing until you have paid off your consumer debt (credit cards, car loans and student loans). It does not make sense to continue to pay money in interest if you have money in the bank. Once you have gotten out of debt you need to spread the risk out over your investments. Do not make large purchases of one single stock, because you could lose everything if the company were to go out of business.

I disagree partly about not investing until you’ve paid off debt.

In the case of many people (including myself), I believe it is advantageous to strike a balance between paying back student loans and investing in something such as a 401k or a Roth IRA.

If you pay attention to your finances, in the long term you may make more money because you’ve had that much longer to invest (compounding interest).

Also, student loan debt is low interest and you can deduct up to $2500 a year in interest paid.

I can’t afford (ironically) to wait 4 to 5 years to pay off my student loans to start to invest in my retirement.

Additionally, for young persons such as myself (I’m 24), now is a particularly good time to invest, as the market is down (substantially) and I have lots of time to ride out before it’s time for me to cash out.

Then again, if I had 10,000 in credit card bills, car payments, etc, I would certainly agree that you should pay those off first before investing.
