Nadya Suleman must be kidding!

BY Patrick Huguenin
DAILY NEWS STAFF WRITER

Tuesday, February 3rd 2009, 2:37 AM

Business Wire
Kaiser Permanente's 52 member labor and delivery team for the octuplets.

‘Miracle.”

That was the word used a week ago to describe the successful birth of octuplets to Nadya Suleman, 33, a California woman praised by doctors for her strength and courage in the delivery room.

It was only the second time in U.S. history that octuplets have survived.

But by the weekend, there was a new way to describe Suleman’s unbelievable feat: out of control.
The details of Nadya Suleman’s pregnancy paint a picture of a woman with an irrational, seemingly pathological, need to conceive. More than that, they point to severe lapses of judgment in a branch of medicine that is steadily growing more successful and more popular.

As details emerged, Suleman’s story transformed from a dream to a nightmare. Unmarried and with no apparent means of support, she lives with her divorced parents in a three-bedroom house in Whittier, a suburb of Los Angeles.

Last year, her mother, Angela Suleman, filed for bankruptcy after a real estate deal gone wrong.
Most shockingly, Nadya Suleman already had six kids, including 2-year-old twins. All 14 were conceived through in vitro fertilization (IVF).

“I wish it happens to you people,” Suleman’s father, Ed, told reporters outside the family home last Friday, “so you go through hell.”

Success rates for IVF have significantly improved in recent years. The most recent data available to the Society for Reproductive Assisted Technology show that in 2006, 39% of treatments in women under 35 resulted in pregnancies. Of those, 34.3% of IVF-treated mothers in this age group gave birth to twins, triplets or more.

At the same time, New York clinics have reported a rise in interest. The number of IVF cycles given to women at ­Weill Cornell’s Center for Reproductive Medicine & Fertility rose 13% from 2003 to 2006, while at the Columbia University Center for Women’s Reproductive Care, treatments jumped a whopping 49% over the same period.

From 2001 to 2007, the percent of New York babies born to mothers over 40 increased 0.7%.
But although the IVF success rate is ­rising, the risks are still real. That medical professionals could have helped Suleman conceive such a massive brood points to a disregard, some experts say, for common sense and the safety of both mother and children.

Dr. R. Dale McClure, president of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM), told the Daily News that when it comes to IVF treatment, incidents like the Suleman octuplets are far from a desired outcome.

ASRM’s guidelines indicate that only one fertilized embryo should be implanted per patient. If the woman has faced medical challenges to conception, two may be used — but never eight.
In addition, Nadya Suleman had no trouble conceiving. On the contrary, she’d used IVF with success before. So there would have been no reason for doctors to implant her with a number of embryos so wildly outside the guidelines.
