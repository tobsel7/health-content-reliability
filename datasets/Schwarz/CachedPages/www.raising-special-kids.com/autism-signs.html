Early Warning Autism Signs



Autism Signs

These are some things to look for as your child develops from about 6 to 24 months, that may give early warning or be an autism sign. I want to make sure that you understand that this is only a list of developmental indicators and should be used as a guide to ask your pediatrician questions about your child's developmental progress. Please do not diagnose children  yourself - unless you are a doctor!

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a Pervasive Developmental Disorder (PDD) and so developmental milestones are good indicators there may be a problem and you should start asking questions and seeking answers.

I know a good many doctors will brush off your concerns and tell you that all children develop at different rates - they are right, of course - but you, the parent, just KNOW when something isn't right, so if you keep seeing the same (or new ones!) problems, keep asking your child's primary care physician (PCP) and if you keep getting put off - get a second opinion (or third opinion!), or ask for a referral to a specialist "just to be sure."

Early Warning Autism Signs

Here are some early warning autism signs and things to look for at specific ages:

6 Months
Not making eye contact with parents during interaction
Not cooing or babbling
Not smiling when parents smile
not participating in vocal turn-taking (making sounds back and forth - you make a sound - baby makes a sound)
Not responding to pee-a-boo game

12 Months
No attempts at speech
Not pointing, waving or grasping
No response when name is called
Indifferent to others
Repetitive body motions such as rocking or hand flapping
Fixation on a single object
Over sensitivity to textures, smells, sounds
Strong resistance to change in routine
Any loss of language

24 Months
Does not initiate two word phrases
Any loss of words or developmental skills



Regression

A child may just simply not progress developmentally, but it is also possible for a child to develop normally for months or even the first few years and then suddenly become withdrawn, aggressive or just be unable to do things they had previously been able to do. Every child is different, and every case shows up differently, so don't fall for the "well my child has autism and they never do THAT!"

If you are concerned, ask your doctor. But children do develop at different rates, and in different ways, so use this information to investigate further and as a source to come up with good questions to ask your doctor. Jumping to conclusions is NOT good exercise!

A Solid Diagnosis

Keep in mind, that it can take years to get a good diagnosis for your child - and consequently get help that actually helps! I know you didn't want to hear that, and it may not turn out that way for you, but you do need to be prepared.

Our daughter, who is ASD and Bipolar, took 13 years before we could get a solid diagnosis, but was showing autism signs since, well, birth. And in the meantime we have had to deal with everything from "there is nothing wrong with her" to "she is a victim of trauma (PTSD) to it is all our fault - even got turned in to Child Protective Services (CPS) by the doctors we trusted to help.

Finally, we were able to take her to the Autism Clinic at Western Michigan University (it is closed now - but check your local universities for a similar program) and after an extensive look into the situation, they finally gave us a definitive diagnosis and started us on the way to getting the help that she really needed all these years.

As the prevelence of autism grows in the general population (currently estimated at 1 case for every 150 children), and more people and doctors become aware of autism signs and symptoms, I do trust it will be easier to get help for your child with an early diagnosis. If you see any of these indicators or autism signs in your child, or your child starts regressing in development (especially in language and social skills) then start asking questions - and don't stop until you get answers!
