Obama's birthplace? Never mind

Published: Friday, December 05, 2008, 4:30 AM     Updated: Friday, December 05, 2008, 4:32 AM
Kevin OBrien, The Plain Dealer
Follow
Share this story
Story tools

A reader wrote in today, asking my opinion about a couple of lawsuits still wending their way through the federal courts, questioning whether Barack Obama meets the constitutional requirement that a president be a natural-born American citizen.

This isn't a bandwagon I can ever see myself getting on. Obama ran out the clock on this issue -- if, indeed, there is anything to this issue -- and the people elected him. I believe that tearing the country apart over what, at the end of the day, is a technicality would be a terrible mistake.

0

The Republicans should have run a better candidate and a better campaign. They didn't and the people chose Obama. This desire to wrangle endlessly over the election is sadly reminiscent of disappointed liberals' tantrums after the 2000 and 2004 elections. It may not be beneath the dignity of Republican partisans, but it ought to be beneath the dignity of principled conservatives. Rending the fabric of the Republic for political gain is not something conservatives should ever do. That shouldn't even be a temptation for them.

It is important that people who love America be faithful to the Constitution AND the long-term health of the nation. In this case, that requires not pushing this question of Obama's birthplace. People who are truly concerned about this as a purely constitutional question without regard to Obama's own status -- if, indeed, there are any such people -- should push instead for a federal law that lays out what constitutes sufficient proof of a potential presidential candidate's natural-born American status and when such proof must be made available to the public. That would give that section of the Constitution teeth that it currently lacks.

Whether or not that statute ever gets written, conservatives should concentrate their energies on building a better case for themselves within the Republican Party and with the American people, so that there is a recognizably conservative alternative in the next election -- something we lacked this year at the presidential level. And rather than squandering their resistance on an election that is OVER, they should marshal it to explain the implications of Obama's socialist ideas and defeat them when they appear as proposed legislation.

For the last 100 years, America has experienced a trend toward socialism. At some point, it would be helpful to recognize that drift for what it is and have a real debate about whether we're going to allow it to continue. At some point, even if we could oust Obama on a technicality, we're going to have a president whose policies are so clearly redistributionist and totalitarian that he's going to force that debate. I'd rather have it now, rather than after two or three or four more decades of incremental, seemingly innocuous drift to the left. The more time and distance we put between ourselves and the ideals of Jefferson and Madison, the harder it's going to be to make any headway in reining in the state and restoring the rights of individuals.

Obama won. I no longer care where he was born. I don't care what he said during the campaign. I don't care what he promised to do. I don't care what kind of person he is. What I care about now -- the one and only thing -- is engaging the proposals he actually puts forward, holding his ideas up to intellectual and constitutional scrutiny, and demonstrating why other ideas are preferable. It's not the man that matters; it's the ideas. Presidents come and go, but ideas have long-lasting, far-reaching effects. History shows that there aren't many things more dangerous than a leader who has a strong appeal and weak ideas. But it's always the ideas that really matter, and that's where conservatives need to keep their focus for the next four years.

You've missed a couple important points, Mr. O'Brien.

1. Obama is about to take this oath: "I do solemnly swear (or affirm) that I will faithfully execute the office of President of the United States, and will to the best of my ability, preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution of the United States."

If he knows that he is ineligible according to the Constitution to serve as President of the United States, and still allows himself to become such, his oath of office would essentially be null and void, as his presidency would be foundationally against the Constitution.

2. If the Constitution needs to be changed in order to preserve the long-term health of the nation, so be it, but until then, it should stand.

One thing conservatives despise is when court justices "legislate from the bench" - when they rule on cases using as the basis for their decisions not just the Constitution, but their own ideas, interpretations, feelings, and opinions (case in point being Roe v. Wade).

3. The general election/popularity contest has taken place, yes, but the electoral college doesn't meet for another 10 days. Do the electors not also have the responsibility to uphold the Constitution?


I wrote you and several other editors and columnists earlier this week. My request was not that any of you agree with the basis for the lawsuits at all. All I'm asking is that the Plain Dealer cover the story. The lawsuits have been taking place at least since August, and at least one of them, Donofrio v. Wells, has been distributed for conference today (meaning the Supreme Court will decide whether or not to hear the case).

What's the worst that can happen if the Plain Dealer covers it and nothing comes of it? Not much. Perhaps a few people would be annoyed that the paper would cover such a "nonissue."

However, if the Supreme Court does hear the case and, heaven forbid, if Obama happens to be ineligible, what kind of effects will we experience if the whole thing explodes at once? I fear rioting would ensue. At the very least, people would be (understandably) angry that they didn't hear about it sooner.
