Is now a good time to invest in oil?

The US Treasury's bid to bail out the US economy has failed to convince sending the price of crude oil lower.

 

By Cherry Reynard
Published: 3:52PM BST 23 Sep 2008

Oil price falls for first time in a week

The prospect of increased US government debt and the resultant weakening of the dollar sent investors in search of 'real assets' of which oil was the main beneficiary. However, despite rising 20 per cent in over the past few days the price of crude has fallen below the $100 a barrel mark.

Does the the US intervention represent a resumption of a long term bull trend for the oil price?

Investors have made good money from oil and other natural resources up to the beginning of this year, but recent performance has been hit by fears of receding demand in the face of a global economic slowdown. Oil shares, which had been one of the most resilient areas of the stock market, have been knocked back. OPEC agreed to pull back supply at its last meeting to shore up the price, which – until this week – had been largely ineffective.

Stuart Connell, co-portfolio manager on the JP Morgan Natural Resources fund, says that the latest rise is short-term. While this doesn't rule out a return to the bull market for oil, he says: "This latest move up is a short-term squeeze rather than a fundamental move in the price. The longer-term prices remain lower." November futures contracts are substantially below the current headline price of oil.

The drivers behind the initial fall in prices had more substance. Craig Heron, fund manager at New Star, says a number of factors were at work: "There was a lot of speculation during that last leg upwards. Then, Saudi Arabia started producing more oil, demand started to tail off as the global economy weakened and there was simply very little rationale for the oil price to keep going up."

The US remains the biggest consumer of oil, but demand has been declining for several years. A combination of the economic slowdown and high oil prices forced changes in behaviour. Car sales slowed, particularly in the gas-guzzling SUV market, and usage fell. Equally, the growth in China, India, the Middle East and other emerging markets had created the marginal demand for oil and doubts about short-term growth in these regions put downward pressure on the oil price.

What is likely to happen next?

Any discussion of the future direction of the oil price comes with the caveat that the oil price is notoriously hard to predict. Connell says: "The vast majority of production comes out of the Middle East and we don't have a solid handle on what is going on there. Some have even attempted to use satellite imaging techniques to investigate actual production levels. This is totally different to, say, the metals market where we know most of the major companies that manage projects."

The price of oil over the longer-term will be governed by supply and demand. On the demand side, the key driver for oil is likely to be the ongoing strength of emerging markets. Robin Batchelor, manager of the BlackRock World Energy fund, says: "In the short-term, there is a cyclical slowdown in emerging markets, but the question is how bad that gets. For the time being, that is difficult to predict because we simply don't have the data. The Olympics will skew things in the short-term. This all adds to the uncertainty surrounding the direction of the oil price."

However, Nicholas Brooks, Head of Research and Investment Strategy at ETF Securities believes there will be no derailment of emerging market growth and that the current uncertainty will end as the slowdown in developed markets ends. He says: "It will turn when investors perceive that we have reached the bottom. Then people will start to look forward." Connell agrees: "We're still very bullish on China and the demand for commodities."

Growth in emerging markets is a far more important swing factor than growth in the US. Demand for oil from the US has been steadily falling, but as Gary Dugan, chief investment officer at Merrill Lynch Global Wealth Management, says: "China is still growing at 9-10 per cent per year. Demand for oil is growing in line with its growth rate. Even if demand in the US is negative, overall market demand can still grow."

Supply is a trickier beast to predict, particularly with OPEC's recent unexpected cut. The cut is relatively small at 520,000 barrels per day, but for many who thought that supply was already tight, it has come as a surprise.

But what does this mean for the actual price of oil?

Guinness says oil is in a long-term bull market, which could last another five to seven years until it reaches a price at which demand is choked off. However, he believes the oil price may not yet have touched its lows and may go down to $80 a barrel temporarily. The average yearly price of oil has been rising in a consistent way since 2002 and he believes these rises will continue, but the oil price needs a period at a lower price to bring this year's average down to normalised levels. Guinness adds: "Investors should focus on the fact that the oil price is still trading up. It is very significantly above where it was 5-6 years ago."

Heron also believes that the oil price may dip as low as $80 in the short-term, but this remains a blip in long-term bull market. He believes the most unlikely scenario is any immediate return to $140 per barrel.

Whall believes the oil price will find its floor at around $100 before starting to recover. He adds: "The Chinese will buy everything at that price. It is proving very difficult to maintain supply. I don't believe you will ever see resources priced as cheaply as they are now." It is difficult to make a case for oil returning to $140 a barrel and beyond in the short-term. However, the consensus appears to be that oil will not go significantly lower than its current levels, particularly given the recent moves by OPEC.

How can I make money from a rising oil price?

Investors have a number of choices when seeking to take advantage of any future rises in the oil price: They can invest directly in the shares of the oil majors and production/service companies; They can invest in funds that look at the oil sector; Or they can invest directly in the oil price via an exchange-traded fund (ETF).

Oil shares do not move directly in line with the oil price, because there are other factors at work such as management skill, debt or distribution. However, there is a correlation over the longer-term. By most measures, oil shares look cheap. They have only partially participated in the phenomenal growth in the oil price of the past few years.

Bachelor has been a buyer of oil stocks recently. He says: "The baby has been thrown out with the bathwater in recent markets. In this market we believe the big oil majors such as Exxon and BP will perform best. They are implementing large scale share buy-backs. When the market turns, however, we believe some of the smaller exploration and production companies will win out. So for the time being, we have a bit of both." In general, he likes companies with exposure to Brazil and India, but is less keen on the Russian story.

On the funds side, Heron is holding specific exposure to the sector through the Zurich-run Earth Energy fund. However, he also likes Guinness's Global Energy fund after his strong track record managing a similar fund at Investec. Heron says that the Schroders and Invesco energy funds are also good. However, he says that investors seeking exposure to the oil price need to make sure their fund invests in that area. Most do, but it will vary.
