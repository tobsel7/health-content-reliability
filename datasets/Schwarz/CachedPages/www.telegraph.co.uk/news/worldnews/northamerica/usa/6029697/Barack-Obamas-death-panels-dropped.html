Barack Obama's 'death panels' dropped

President Barack Obama's Democratic allies have dropped a controversial proposal for "end of life" discussions with doctors after Republican claims that Americans would face "death panels".

 

By Toby Harnden in Washington
Published: 4:37PM BST 14 Aug 2009

Sarah Palin the former Alaska governor and 2012 White House hopeful, had called the plans "evil".

Senator Chuck Grassley of Iowa, the top Republican on the Senate Finance Committee, said in a statement that the provision had been dropped from consideration by Democrats because it could be misinterpreted or implemented incorrectly.

Mr Grassley had earlier suggested that the National Health Service in Britain would refuse to treat a terminally ill patient such as Senator Edward Kennedy, 77, who is battling brain cancer.

A health care bill passed by three House of Representatives committees allowed doctors to be reimbursed for voluntary counselling sessions about "end of life" decisions.

Mrs Palin stated this could lead to "death panels" and euthanasia.

There is nothing in any of the bills on Capitol Hill that give government any power to end care for the critically ill in order to save money.

But the false claim that a provision for euthanasia was being slipped into the small print of a bill has been a potent one. Outside a public meeting held by Mr Obama in Portsmouth, New Hampshire this week there were signs saying: "Obama lies, Grandma dies".

Mr Obama was forced to address the issue head on, saying that no one would "pull the plug on Grandma because we've decided that it's too expensive to let her live anymore" and adding: "Somehow, it has got spun into this idea of death panels, I am not in favour of that, I want to clear the air here."
